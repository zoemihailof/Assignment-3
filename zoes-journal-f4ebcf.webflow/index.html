Week four marked further exploration of the history of web design. This period, spanning the late 20th and early 21st centuries, was a time of rapid technological advancement, and it was particularly intriguing to learn about key innovations that have had a profound impact on the design industry.

In addition to this, our class also focused on customer journey maps. At each stage, customers engage in specific activities that shape their overall experience. What makes journey mapping even more insightful is the inclusion of emotions and experiences that customers feel throughout their interaction. These emotions have a direct influence on how customers perceive a product or service. 

Customer journey mapping is a great tool for designers seeking to improve the user experience. By gaining a deep understanding of how customers move through each stage, designers can identify pain points, areas of delight, and opportunities for enhancement. This information allows designers to create more user-centric and impactful designs that resonate with the target audience.

WEEK 7

COMPOSITION & ASSIGNMENT SUBMISSION

WEEK 7-BLOG REFLECTION

Week 7 proved to be an important point in our journey of mastering web design. This time, our focus shifted towards the importance of composition and the essential elements that shape the user experience on a website. The lecture and class delved deep into several aspects that are integral in creating visually appealing and functional web designs. 

One of the core themes of the week was "composition." This encompassed various elements, each playing a crucial role in how we structure and present information on a webpage. We explored topics such as grids, visual hierarchy, colour and typography, scale, white space, and balance. These elements are like the building blocks of an effective website layout, and understanding how to use them cohesively can have a positive impact on user engagement and overall aesthetics. The week's lessons reinforced the importance of not only what we place on a page but also how we arrange it. 

Moving beyond the visual aspects of composition, the class then delved into the concept of "affordances." Affordances are essentially cues or signals that tell users how to interact with various elements on a webpage. This includes buttons, links, call-to-action elements, input fields, icons, and even the language we use in UX (User Experience) copy. Understanding and utilizing affordances effectively is paramount in ensuring that users can easily navigate a website and perform the desired actions. A well-designed affordance provides users with a clear indication of what to do and how to do it. 

This week's focus on composition and affordances served as a valuable reminder that web design is not just about aesthetics; it's about creating a seamless and intuitive user experience. It's about guiding users through the content, facilitating their interactions, and ultimately achieving the goals of the website, whether it's making a purchase, signing up for a newsletter, or accessing information.

 As I reflect on this week's lessons, I'm increasingly aware of the multifaceted nature of web design. It's a blend of creativity, psychology, and functionality. It's about finding the right balance between visual appeal and user-centered design. I look forward to applying these newfound insights in my future web design projects, striving to create not just beautiful websites, but also websites that are a easy for users to explore and engage with. The journey continues, and with each week, I'm one step closer to becoming a more proficient and well-rounded web designer.

WEEK 9

RESPONSIVE DESIGN & LABELLING FILES

WEEK 9-BLOG REFLECTION

For week 9  we delved into the topic of responsive design. In today's digital landscape, where an array of devices and screen sizes are in use, the ability to create adaptable websites is a necessity. Our lesson emphasized the importance of ensuring that the websites we create can adapt to a wide range of devices. The concept of responsive design involves more than just aesthetics; it's about delivering a consistent and user-friendly experience, whether someone is visiting a site from a desktop computer, a tablet, or a smartphone.

 We explored various testing methods to verify the responsiveness of a website.Device emulation allowed us to simulate how a site would appear on different devices, while cross-browser testing ensured compatibility with various web browsers. Usability testing, an essential part of the process, helped identify any issues users might encounter when navigating the site. These testing methods serve as check points in the design and development process, helping us create websites that are truly adaptable and accessible.

 In the second part of the lecture, we dived into the topic of file organization. This is an aspect of web design that might be overlooked but holds significance. Consistent and well-structured file organization is essential for efficiency, collaboration, and maintaining a project's integrity over time. It involves creating organized project folders, naming conventions, effectively managing project assets, and maintaining clear and helpful documentation. These practices contribute to better project management and collaboration. It's an area that I personally recognize I need to improve upon, as it can stream line the design and development process.

 Lastly, the lecture showed the importance of alt text, a concept that carries significant weight in the realm of web accessibility. Writing alt text is not just a best practice; it's a critical component in providing an inclusive user experience. Alt text serves several vital purposes. It provides a textual description of images, making content accessible to those with visual impairments. Additionally, it enhances search engine optimization (SEO) by providing context to search engines. By creating meaningful and descriptive alttext, we ensure that our websites are accessible to a wider audience and can be easily discovered through online searches.
